How Make a Study Plan

  2018-07-03  -    Voylin's Life

How to make a study plan

In this video, I will show you how you can plan out your study schedule. It is also important to make your study schedule flexible so you enjoy (or at least not hate) it. In this article I’ll try to get a bit more in depth with how to make a study plan.

Creating study plans for languages

Learning languages, something many people really look up against. It’s too difficult, too time consuming, not worth it. Those are the things many people say about language learning, especially towards people learning Japanese as most people will never really need the language to begin with. And those things they say may be true, but you can do whatever you want.

Step 1: Set specific goals

Before creating your study plan, it’s important to set specific goals for what you want to achieve. These goals should be clear, measurable, and realistic. For example, a goal could be to achieve a certain level on a language proficiency test, or to be able to have a basic conversation in the language.

Do you want to achieve fluency, or just being able to read, or have conversations? Fluency in any language will take years to master, so start by setting your goals low so you don’t overstress and burn yourself out. Been there, done that, not a pleasant experience.

Step 2: Resources

Once you have set your goals, the next step is to identify the resources you will use to achieve them. Resources can include textbooks, language apps, language learning websites, language exchange programs, and more. It’s important to find resources that align with your learning style and preferences.

Just don’t spend too much time finding the perfect resources. Study from everything you find and only look for new resources once you finished the last one. I have wasted soo much time trying to find the perfect resources which could have been better invested in the studying part itself.

Step 3: Create a schedule

Creating a schedule is a crucial step in staying organized and motivated while learning a new language. Your schedule should include specific times and days for studying, as well as breaks and rewards for reaching milestones.

It’s important to be realistic when creating your schedule and to make sure you have enough time to practice and review. So start by underestimating your schedule, start small and whilst following your schedule, you can start adjusting it more and more towards your daily life. Plan to also create a good balance between work, studying, school,social life and hobbies. It’s not easy and you’ll probably fail to do so in the beginning, but just keep adjusting and eventually it will work out.

Step 4: Track progress

Tracking your progress is an important part of keeping motivated and staying on track. You can track your progress by keeping a journal, using a language learning app, or taking regular assessments to measure your progress. This is something I wished that I did from the start.

If I ever start learning a new language then I will probably want to track everything, the amount of stuff I had to relearn or forgot that I ever saw is quite large. I’m studying Japanese right now and my next language will probably be Korean. The very first day that I start, I will keep an excel sheet to keep track of everything. I will probably make vlogs about it so you can see how that will work out for me at that time.
